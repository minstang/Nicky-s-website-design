## Design Context

### Users
Fellow researchers and PhD students in economics. They visit to understand Nicholas's research specialty, assess publication status (accepted journals, R&Rs, working papers), and gauge scholarly credibility. These are sophisticated readers who value substance over flash — they scan for journal names, co-authors, and paper topics quickly.

### Brand Personality
Intellectual minimalism. Clean, scholarly, quietly confident. The interface should feel like a well-typeset academic journal — not a tech startup, not a portfolio site, not a blog. Think: the calm authority of a beautifully printed monograph. Three words: **restrained, precise, credible**.

### Aesthetic Direction
- **Visual tone**: Minimalist scholar. Typography-driven hierarchy. Let whitespace and type do the heavy lifting — no decorative elements, no cards, no icons beyond what's functional.
- **References**: Justin Marx's portfolio (https://justinmarx.design/) for its typographic confidence and editorial feel. Anthropic's site (https://www.anthropic.com/) for its restrained elegance and clarity.
- **Anti-references**: No glassmorphism. No centered top navigation bars. No startup/tech aesthetics. No Apple-style hero sections. No "good design" tropes that don't serve a scholarly identity.
- **Theme**: Light mode primary. Support dark mode via `prefers-color-scheme` and a manual toggle. Both modes should feel intentional, not just inverted.
- **Color palette**: Keep the existing monochrome + crimson (#990033) foundation. Crimson is used for publication status — it carries meaning and should stay. Tint neutrals subtly toward the crimson hue for cohesion.

### Design Principles
1. **Typography is the design.** Visual hierarchy comes from type scale, weight, and spacing — not color, borders, or decoration. Every level of information (name, title, paper name, journal, abstract) should be instantly distinguishable through type alone.
2. **Content is sacred.** All text, photos, meta tags, paper ordering, and links stay exactly as they are. The redesign is purely a styling exercise.
3. **Quiet confidence over loud design.** The site should feel like it belongs to someone who doesn't need to prove anything — the work speaks for itself. No attention-grabbing tricks.
4. **Scannable structure.** Researchers scan fast. Publication status (accepted, R&R, working paper) and journal names should be immediately visible without reading full titles. Group and differentiate published work from in-progress work.
5. **Respect the reader.** Comfortable reading measure, proper line heights, accessible contrast. The site should be as pleasant to read as a well-set page of text.
